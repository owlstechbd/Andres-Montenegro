Integrated Research Design Prospectus – Andres Montenegro

Section 1: Introduction

A. What is your primary research interest?

My primary research interest is direct feedback as a driver of resilience in leadership. My big picture vision is to make direct feedback a normalized leadership practice—without filters, without euphemisms, and without sugarcoating. I believe everyone, not just current leaders, but coworkers and future leaders too, should be developed through direct, honest input. Feedback should not be labeled positive or negative; it should just be feedback—direct, clear, actionable. When a person does something, good or bad, they should receive feedback. That's how people grow. That's how leaders are forged. Even when someone wins or performs excellently, they still deserve feedback—on what to do next, how to go further, and where the next stretch of development lies. Direct feedback challenges the ego, builds emotional strength, and supports long-term resilience.

B. What is the research problem you are addressing?

We have built a professional culture—especially over the last decade—that avoids directness. We tiptoe around problems. We protect people's feelings instead of their development. In doing so, we have weakened our organizations. I see too many people afraid to say the truth when something is wrong, and afraid to hear it when they have made a mistake. I want to study this fear of directness. I want to uncover what we have lost by abandoning feedback as a tool for growth, and I want to document how leaders can recover that strength—by embracing feedback not as insult, but as insight. That's the heart of my research problem.

Section 2: Conceptual Framework

A. What are some important ideas, concepts, or theories that inform your study?

The most important concepts informing my study are direct feedback, psychological safety, and emotional intelligence. These three pillars form the lens through which I see leadership development and resilience building. Emotional intelligence helps leaders regulate and interpret the discomfort that comes with feedback. Psychological safety determines whether feedback is delivered and received in a productive way. And direct feedback, in itself, is the mechanism that activates growth. These concepts are not abstract for me—they are active forces in how real leaders become stronger in real environments.

B. What are some important theorists and researchers whose work has informed your study?

Goleman's work on emotional intelligence helps me understand how leaders absorb feedback. Edmondson's research on psychological safety explains why some environments allow growth while others suppress it. And the grounded theory methods of Charmaz guide how I build a model from participants' experiences. These scholars helped me take what I have witnessed in the real world and see it as something that can be analyzed, explained, and eventually taught.

C. What ideas from theory and research have helped you clarify your own understanding of your study?

One big idea I have taken from theory is that leaders do not grow from praise—they grow from friction, discomfort, and insight. That only happens through real feedback. I've learned that emotion and environment are just as important as the words being spoken. Without safety and intelligence, feedback can harm instead of help. This realization helped me clarify why direct feedback, given correctly, is a force for good in leadership development.

D. What questions still remain for you?

I want to know why so many leaders avoid giving direct feedback. I want to know what cultural or emotional block stops people from telling the truth at work. And I want to understand how leaders bounce back from the pain of direct feedback and use that pain to build strength. These are the unanswered questions that keep me curious.

Section 3: Research Questions

A. What do you want to learn from your study?

I want to understand what stops people from giving or receiving direct feedback in leadership environments. I want to learn what kind of feedback builds resilience, what conditions make it work, and how emotional intelligence and psychological safety shape the feedback experience. I also want to see what is possible when we stop filtering our words and instead speak truthfully to one another with respect. I want to document how this directness leads to better leadership.

B. Why do you want to learn this?

I want to learn this because I believe we are losing our ability to help one another grow. We have built what I call a house of glass—where nobody wants to say anything real because they are afraid of causing offense. In doing that, we are preventing growth, especially in leadership. I believe feedback does not need to be labeled positive or negative—it just needs to be direct. I know others feel the same, but they are afraid to say it. My study gives voice to that silent group and helps restore feedback as a tool for growth.

Section 4: Research Relationships

A. What relationships do you have with the people you plan to study?

I do not have personal relationships with the people I want to participate with in the future. I have intentionally avoided close friends or colleagues to avoid bias or pressure. The people I have approached are mostly professional acquaintances that I connected with through networks. This distance is by design—I want their responses to be honest, not polite.

B. How will you establish a learning relationship with your participants?

I will establish relationships by emphasizing anonymity, trust, and simplicity. I tell participants that I am not looking for fancy answers—I want truth. I let them know that I respect their privacy and will not ask for names, titles, or affiliations. I've found that this opens up space for people to be more real. I approach the relationship not as a scholar judging them, but as a peer who values their lived experience.

Section 5: Site and Participation Selection

A. Where do you plan to conduct your research?

There is no specific physical site. All data will be collected anonymously, through emailed documents or shared forms. Because the people I am speaking with work in sensitive fields—including government and military—we avoid identifying locations or organizations. The site, if we can call it that, is a secure digital exchange of ideas.

B. How and why have you chosen this site or participants?

I will choose participants with significant professional experience—many have worked in or around government, foreign service, or military. These individuals operate in high-pressure, high-responsibility roles where feedback is often limited or filtered. I have also made a point to include women with senior leadership experience, to see how gender influences the feedback experience. My one challenge is that most participants are over 35, so I am now working to include younger voices as well—ideally aged 20–25—to broaden the generational scope of the study.

Section 6: Data Collection

A. How will you collect your data?

I will collect data through written, anonymous interviews. Participants receive a Microsoft Word document with a short explanation and ten open-ended questions. They respond in writing and return it through email or a private form. I choose this method because it's simple, secure, and works well with people who cannot share identifying information. Many of my participants work in government or sensitive environments, so anonymity is essential. This approach respects their confidentiality and allows for honest responses.

B. Why is this method the best one to answer your research questions?

This method works because it is practical and respectful. I did not want to create a complicated research process—life is already complex, and I needed a system that is sustainable. Collecting data through simple Word documents removes pressure from participants, especially when they are used to dealing with confidentiality. It allows them to reflect before answering. The stories they're sharing are full of emotional weight, and this approach gives them the space to speak freely without fear or formality.

Section 7: Data Analysis Techniques

A. How will you analyze your data?

I will read each interview response closely, line by line, and start labeling patterns and phrases that show meaning—this is called open coding. From there, I will group those codes into larger themes and see how different ideas connect—this is focused coding. Finally, I will look at all the pieces and ask myself, "What is the big idea here?"—this leads to a central theory or model. I am using grounded theory methods from Charmaz, which allows me to find meaning based on what people actually say, not based on what I expect to find.

B. Why is this method best suited to your study?

It fits because I don't want to force my ideas onto the data. I want the data to speak. People are telling real stories about leadership, fear, growth, and resilience. The best way to honor their stories is to build meaning from their words. Grounded theory lets me build from the ground up, rather than squeeze answers into a box. This also aligns with my goal to create a usable model of how feedback shapes leadership in real-world environments.

Section 8: Validity Issues

A. What threats to validity do you foresee in your study?

The biggest threat is over-interpreting or reading too much into people's stories. Because the feedback experiences people share are emotional, it would be easy for me to project my own views. Another issue is that some participants may hold back out of habit or fear, even though the process is anonymous.

B. What strategies and techniques will you use to ensure that your study is trustworthy and valid?

My strategy is to keep things simple. Sit down, read carefully, listen deeply, and stay honest about what the data is showing. I will document my thoughts as I go so I can track my thinking. If I am unsure, I will ask a peer to read through it with me. I want the analysis to be clear, respectful, and useful. I agree with what Simon Sinek said once—if you make things too complex, people stop listening. So I will keep the process real, human, and understandable.

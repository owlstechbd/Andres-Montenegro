Personal Leadership Development Plan

The Personal Leadership Development Plan is structured around four core areas: Resilience Development, Emotional Intelligence, Psychological Safety, and Direct Feedback. The objective is to integrate these principles into daily leadership practices, creating an environment where both leaders and team members feel empowered to contribute effectively.

Resilience Development

Focus on strengthening decision-making under pressure by implementing strategies that promote adaptive thinking and maintaining composure in high-stakes situations.

Psychological Safety

Establish a culture where team members feel safe to express ideas, share concerns, and offer constructive feedback without fear of retaliation or judgment. This includes promoting open dialogue, encouraging diverse perspectives, and creating an inclusive atmosphere that values input from all team members.

Emotional Intelligence

Develop the ability to manage emotions effectively, fostering deeper connections with team members and enhancing communication in diverse settings.

Direct Feedback

Utilize structured feedback sessions to provide clear, actionable insights that drive personal and professional growth, ensuring that feedback is delivered in a way that is constructive, supportive, and aimed at fostering continuous improvement.

This development plan is designed to cultivate a leadership style that not only prioritizes strategic decision-making but also emphasizes creating a psychologically safe workplace where innovation, trust, and growth are actively encouraged.

Year 1 Reflection - Leadership Development Journey

Doctor of Executive Leadership Program - University of Charleston, WV

Foundation and Context: Military Leadership Meets Academic Framework

Entering the Doctor of Executive Leadership program marked a profound transition from operational military leadership to academic exploration of leadership theory and practice. My eighteen years of military service, including specialized operations and international experience with the French Foreign Legion, provided a unique lens through which to examine and challenge traditional leadership paradigms. Year one became a critical period of deconstructing previously held assumptions about leadership while simultaneously building a more nuanced, research-based understanding of what it means to lead effectively in complex, dynamic environments.

The military context had instilled in me a command-and-control mindset that emphasized hierarchy, rapid decision-making under pressure, and results-oriented approaches to team management. However, the DEL program immediately challenged this framework by introducing concepts that required a more collaborative, emotionally intelligent, and psychologically aware approach to leadership. This cognitive dissonance became the foundation for significant personal and professional growth throughout the first year.

Theoretical Frameworks and Paradigm Shifts

The introduction to contemporary leadership theories created a fundamental shift in my understanding of effective leadership practice. Transformational leadership theory, particularly James MacGregor Burns' original conceptualization and Bass's subsequent refinements, provided a framework that resonated deeply with my military experience while challenging me to evolve beyond transactional approaches. The emphasis on inspiring followers to transcend their own self-interests for the good of the organization aligned with military values of service and sacrifice, yet the methods for achieving this transformation required a complete reimagining of my leadership approach.

Adaptive leadership, as conceptualized by Ronald Heifetz and Marty Linsky, became particularly relevant given my background in special operations where adaptability under extreme conditions was essential for survival and mission success. However, the academic exploration of adaptive leadership revealed layers of complexity I had not previously considered. The distinction between technical problems (which have known solutions) and adaptive challenges (which require learning, experimentation, and often fundamental changes in values, beliefs, or behaviors) provided a new lens for analyzing leadership challenges I had encountered throughout my military career.

The concept of distributed leadership challenged my deeply ingrained beliefs about hierarchical command structures. In military contexts, clear chains of command are not merely organizational preferences but operational necessities that can mean the difference between mission success and catastrophic failure. However, the DEL program introduced me to research demonstrating that distributed leadership approaches often produce superior outcomes in complex, knowledge-intensive environments. This created a productive tension that forced me to examine when and how different leadership approaches might be most effective.

Emotional Intelligence: The Missing Dimension

Perhaps the most transformative aspect of year one was the deep exploration of emotional intelligence as a leadership competency. Daniel Goleman's framework of self-awareness, self-regulation, motivation, empathy, and social skills provided a systematic approach to understanding and developing capabilities that had been largely intuitive in my military experience. The military had certainly required emotional regulation under extreme stress, but the academic study of emotional intelligence revealed the deliberate, systematic nature of emotional leadership competencies.

Self-awareness became a particular focus of development. The program included extensive self-assessment tools, including the Emotional Quotient Inventory (EQ-i 2.0), 360-degree feedback instruments, and structured reflection exercises that revealed patterns in my emotional responses and leadership behaviors. The results were both validating and challenging. While my military experience had developed strong self-regulation capabilities under pressure, the assessments revealed gaps in emotional self-awareness, particularly in understanding how my emotional states affected others and how subtle emotional cues from team members might inform leadership decisions.

The development of empathy as a leadership competency required significant personal work. Military training emphasizes mission accomplishment and troop welfare, but the systematic development of empathetic understanding—the ability to truly comprehend and share the feelings of others—required new skills and perspectives. This was particularly challenging given the cultural context of military service, where emotional expression is often viewed as weakness or unprofessionalism. The DEL program provided a safe space to explore how empathetic leadership could enhance rather than compromise mission effectiveness.

Psychological Safety: Creating Space for Excellence

The introduction of Amy Edmondson's research on psychological safety represented one of the most practically relevant concepts encountered in year one. Psychological safety—the belief that one can speak up without risk of punishment or humiliation—directly challenged aspects of traditional military culture while simultaneously explaining why some military units consistently outperformed others. The concept provided a framework for understanding how leaders can create environments where team members contribute their best thinking, admit mistakes quickly, and engage in the kind of open dialogue essential for learning and adaptation.

Implementing psychological safety concepts required careful consideration of context and culture. In military environments, certain types of psychological safety are essential for operational effectiveness—team members must feel comfortable reporting equipment malfunctions, tactical concerns, or personal limitations that could affect mission success. However, military discipline and hierarchy create natural tensions with psychological safety. The academic exploration of this concept provided tools for creating psychological safety within appropriate boundaries, allowing for enhanced team performance while maintaining necessary discipline and structure.

The practical application of psychological safety principles in my current work environment became an immediate focus. I began experimenting with creating regular opportunities for team input on operational procedures, implementing structured after-action reviews that emphasized learning over blame, and modeling vulnerability by openly discussing my own mistakes and learning processes. The results were immediately apparent in increased team engagement, more innovative problem-solving approaches, and improved overall team performance.

Leadership Authenticity and Values Alignment

Year one included extensive exploration of authentic leadership theory, which emphasizes self-awareness, relational transparency, balanced processing, and an internalized moral perspective. This framework provided tools for examining the alignment between personal values, leadership behaviors, and organizational outcomes. My military experience had instilled strong values around service, integrity, and commitment, but the academic exploration of authentic leadership challenged me to examine how these values translated into specific leadership behaviors and decisions.

The process of values clarification revealed both strengths and areas for development. Military service had reinforced values of duty, honor, and country, but the transition to civilian contexts required examining how these foundational values could be expressed through leadership behaviors that were appropriate and effective in different organizational cultures. This process involved considerable reflection on how to maintain personal authenticity while adapting leadership approaches to different contexts and cultures.

The concept of moral leadership became particularly relevant given my military background. The program explored how leaders can maintain ethical clarity while navigating complex organizational dynamics, competing stakeholder interests, and ambiguous situations. This was familiar territory from military service, where ethical decision-making under pressure is a constant requirement, but the academic framework provided new tools for systematic ethical analysis and decision-making.

Resilience and Adaptive Capacity

Resilience, both personal and organizational, emerged as a central theme throughout year one. The military had certainly developed personal resilience capabilities, but the academic exploration of resilience provided a more comprehensive understanding of how leaders can systematically develop both their own adaptive capacity and that of their organizations.

Personal resilience assessment revealed both strengths and development opportunities. Military training and experience had developed strong capabilities in areas such as stress tolerance, emotional regulation under pressure, and rapid adaptation to changing circumstances. However, the academic framework revealed additional dimensions of resilience, including cognitive flexibility, social support utilization, and meaning-making capabilities that could be further developed.

Communication and Influence

Year one included extensive exploration of communication theory and influence strategies that extended far beyond the command authority that had been my primary tool in military contexts. Robert Cialdini's principles of influence provided a framework for understanding how leaders can effectively influence others without relying solely on positional authority.

Storytelling emerged as a particularly powerful communication tool that had been underutilized in my leadership practice. The program explored how leaders can use narrative to create shared meaning, inspire action, and build emotional connections with team members. This was a significant departure from military communication norms but proved highly effective in civilian leadership contexts.

Systems Thinking and Complexity Leadership

The introduction to systems thinking provided a framework for understanding leadership challenges in terms of interconnected relationships, feedback loops, and emergent properties rather than simple cause-and-effect relationships. This was particularly relevant given my background in special operations, where mission success often depended on understanding and navigating complex systems with multiple interdependent variables.

Peter Senge's work on learning organizations provided tools for understanding how leaders can create systems that continuously adapt and improve. The five disciplines—personal mastery, mental models, shared vision, team learning, and systems thinking—provided a comprehensive framework for organizational development that could be applied across different contexts and cultures.

Cultural Competence and Global Leadership

My international military experience, including service with the French Foreign Legion, provided unique perspectives on cultural differences in leadership expectations and effectiveness. Year one included systematic exploration of cultural intelligence and global leadership competencies that provided frameworks for understanding and leveraging cultural differences in leadership contexts.

Hofstede's cultural dimensions theory provided tools for analyzing how national and organizational cultures influence leadership effectiveness. The exploration of power distance, individualism versus collectivism, uncertainty avoidance, and other cultural dimensions helped explain patterns I had observed in international military service while providing frameworks for adapting leadership approaches to different cultural contexts.

The development of cultural intelligence required ongoing self-assessment and reflection on cultural assumptions and biases. My multilingual capabilities (Spanish, French, English, with knowledge of Portuguese and Italian) provided advantages in cross-cultural communication, but the program revealed additional dimensions of cultural competence related to non-verbal communication, cultural values, and leadership expectations that required continued development.

Integration and Synthesis: Building a Personal Leadership Philosophy

Year one culminated in the development of a comprehensive personal leadership philosophy that integrated military experience with academic learning. This philosophy emphasized adaptive leadership approaches that could be effectively applied across different contexts while maintaining personal authenticity and values alignment.

The philosophy incorporated several key principles:

Adaptive Authenticity: Maintaining personal values and authenticity while adapting leadership behaviors to different contexts, cultures, and organizational needs. This required developing the capability to express core values through different leadership styles and approaches.

Emotional Intelligence Integration: Systematically developing and applying emotional intelligence competencies to enhance leadership effectiveness. This included ongoing self-assessment, feedback seeking, and skill development in areas such as empathy, social awareness, and relationship management.

Psychological Safety Leadership: Creating environments where team members can contribute their best thinking, admit mistakes, and engage in open dialogue while maintaining appropriate boundaries and performance standards.

Systems-Aware Decision Making: Considering the broader system implications of leadership decisions, including unintended consequences, feedback effects, and long-term sustainability.

Cultural Adaptability: Leveraging cultural intelligence to adapt leadership approaches to different cultural contexts while building bridges across cultural differences.

Challenges and Growth Areas Identified

Year one also revealed several specific areas requiring continued development:

Patience with Ambiguity: Military training emphasizes rapid decision-making and clear action, but many leadership challenges require tolerance for ambiguity and extended periods of uncertainty. Developing comfort with not knowing and not having immediate solutions required conscious effort and practice.

Collaborative Decision-Making: Moving beyond consultative decision-making (where leaders gather input but make final decisions independently) to truly collaborative approaches where decision-making authority is shared required new skills and comfort levels.

Vulnerability and Transparency: While military culture values honesty and integrity, it often discourages displays of vulnerability or uncertainty. Learning to model vulnerability as a leadership strength required conscious practice and feedback.

Long-term Perspective: Military operations often focus on immediate tactical success, but civilian leadership requires longer-term strategic thinking and patience with gradual change processes.

Assessment and Feedback Integration

The systematic integration of feedback throughout year one provided unprecedented insight into leadership strengths and development opportunities. The combination of self-assessment tools, 360-degree feedback, peer evaluations, and faculty observations created a comprehensive picture of leadership effectiveness across different contexts and relationships.

The feedback revealed consistent patterns of strength in areas such as decisiveness under pressure, integrity and ethical behavior, commitment to team success, and ability to maintain performance under stress. These strengths clearly reflected military training and experience but were valuable across all leadership contexts.

Development opportunities identified through feedback included areas such as patience with team learning processes, comfort with shared decision-making authority, and skill in managing conflict through dialogue rather than authority. These areas became specific focuses for continued development throughout the program.

Looking Forward: Foundation for Continued Growth

Year one established a solid foundation for continued leadership development throughout the DEL program. The combination of theoretical learning, practical application, systematic feedback, and reflective practice created a framework for ongoing growth that could be sustained beyond the academic program.

The identification of resilience development through direct feedback as a potential dissertation focus emerged from year one learning and reflection. The integration of military experience with academic learning suggested that structured feedback systems could be powerful tools for developing both individual and organizational resilience capabilities.

The year concluded with a clear sense of direction for continued development and a commitment to integrating academic learning with practical leadership challenges. The foundation established in year one provided confidence that the subsequent years of the program would build effectively on this base while continuing to challenge and expand leadership capabilities.

Personal Transformation and Growth

Perhaps most significantly, year one represented a period of significant personal transformation. The integration of military experience with academic learning created new perspectives on leadership effectiveness and new capabilities for influencing positive change in organizations and communities. The systematic development of emotional intelligence, cultural competence, and systems thinking provided tools for more effective leadership while maintaining the values and commitment that had been developed through military service.

This transformation was not about abandoning military leadership capabilities but rather about expanding and refining them for application in broader contexts. The result was a more comprehensive, nuanced, and effective approach to leadership that honored past experience while embracing new learning and growth opportunities.

Year 2 Reflection - Leadership Development Journey

Doctor of Executive Leadership Program - University of Charleston, WV

Transition from Theory to Praxis: The Integration Challenge

Year two of the Doctor of Executive Leadership program represented a fundamental shift from theoretical exploration to practical integration and application. While year one had focused on deconstructing existing leadership assumptions and building new theoretical frameworks, year two challenged me to synthesize these learnings into coherent, actionable leadership practices while simultaneously laying the groundwork for doctoral research. This dual focus created a dynamic tension between immediate practical application and longer-term research development that proved both challenging and transformative.

The military background that had informed my year one learning continued to provide valuable perspective, but year two required moving beyond the adaptation of military leadership principles to the creation of entirely new leadership approaches suited to complex civilian organizational contexts. This evolution demanded not just intellectual understanding but embodied change—the development of new leadership habits, reflexes, and capabilities that could be deployed naturally and effectively in real-time leadership situations.

Advanced Leadership Theory Integration

Year two introduced more sophisticated theoretical frameworks that required deeper integration with practical experience. The exploration of complexity theory and its application to leadership challenged fundamental assumptions about control, predictability, and linear cause-and-effect relationships that had been central to military leadership training.

Adaptive leadership theory, introduced in year one, became a central focus for practical application. Ronald Heifetz's distinction between technical problems and adaptive challenges provided a lens for analyzing current leadership challenges in my work environment. The systematic application of adaptive leadership principles—getting on the balcony, identifying the adaptive challenge, regulating distress, maintaining disciplined attention, giving work back to the people, and protecting voices of leadership from below—became daily practices that fundamentally changed how I approached organizational challenges.

The integration of complexity leadership theory with practical experience revealed the limitations of traditional command-and-control approaches in contemporary organizational contexts. Dave Snowden's Cynefin framework provided tools for categorizing different types of problems and selecting appropriate leadership responses. Simple problems called for best practice approaches, complicated problems required good practice and expert analysis, complex problems demanded emergent practices and experimentation, while chaotic problems required rapid response and pattern recognition.

This theoretical framework proved immediately applicable to organizational challenges. Rather than defaulting to directive leadership approaches for all problems, I began systematically analyzing the nature of each challenge and adapting leadership responses accordingly. Complex problems, which were more common than I had initially recognized, required leadership approaches that were comfortable with ambiguity, experimentation, and emergent solutions.

Emotional Intelligence Mastery and Application

Building on year one's foundation in emotional intelligence theory, year two focused on advanced application and mastery of emotional leadership competencies. The systematic development of emotional intelligence moved beyond self-awareness to sophisticated understanding of group emotional dynamics and the leader's role in managing collective emotional states.

Daniel Goleman's research on primal leadership and emotional contagion provided frameworks for understanding how leader emotional states ripple through organizations. This was particularly relevant given my military background, where leader emotional regulation under pressure was essential but often focused on individual rather than collective emotional dynamics.

The practical application of emotional intelligence principles required significant behavior change. Military culture had taught emotional regulation through suppression and compartmentalization, but civilian leadership contexts required more sophisticated approaches to emotional expression and management. Learning to appropriately express vulnerability, uncertainty, and even frustration in ways that enhanced rather than diminished leadership effectiveness required conscious practice and feedback.

The development of social awareness capabilities became particularly important as I worked to create psychologically safe environments within my current organizational context. Understanding subtle emotional cues from team members, recognizing unspoken concerns or resistance, and adapting leadership approaches based on emotional feedback became increasingly natural and effective.

Psychological Safety Implementation and Refinement

Amy Edmondson's research on psychological safety, introduced in year one, became a central focus for practical implementation in year two. The challenge was not just understanding the concept intellectually but creating systematic approaches for building and maintaining psychological safety within existing organizational cultures that might not naturally support such environments.

The implementation of psychological safety principles required careful attention to organizational context and culture. In my current work environment, which maintains some military-influenced hierarchical structures, creating psychological safety required finding ways to encourage open dialogue and mistake-sharing while maintaining necessary accountability and performance standards.

Specific practices developed during year two included structured team reflection sessions, systematic error analysis focused on learning rather than blame, regular one-on-one check-ins designed to surface concerns and ideas, and modeling vulnerability by openly discussing my own mistakes and learning processes. The implementation of these practices required consistent attention and refinement based on team feedback and observed outcomes.

The measurement of psychological safety became an important skill development area. Rather than relying on assumptions about team comfort levels, I developed systematic approaches for assessing psychological safety through observation, direct feedback, and anonymous survey mechanisms. This data-driven approach allowed for continuous refinement of approaches and demonstrated measurable improvements in team performance and engagement.

Advanced Communication and Influence Strategies

Year two required moving beyond basic communication competencies to sophisticated understanding of influence, persuasion, and communication across different organizational levels and contexts. The integration of neuroscience research on decision-making and influence provided new tools for understanding how leaders can effectively communicate in ways that align with how people actually process information and make decisions.

The exploration of narrative leadership became particularly important. Stories and metaphors proved powerful tools for creating shared understanding, building emotional connections, and inspiring action in ways that traditional military communication approaches could not achieve. The systematic development of storytelling capabilities required practice and feedback but proved highly effective in civilian leadership contexts.

Advanced influence strategies moved beyond Robert Cialdini's basic principles to more sophisticated understanding of how to build consensus, manage resistance, and create sustainable behavior change. The exploration of motivational interviewing techniques provided tools for helping people discover their own reasons for change rather than trying to convince them through logical arguments or authority.

Cross-cultural communication became increasingly important as my work involved diverse teams with different cultural backgrounds and communication preferences. The application of cultural intelligence frameworks to communication required ongoing attention and adaptation but proved essential for effective leadership in diverse organizational contexts.

Systems Leadership and Organizational Development

Year two introduced advanced concepts in systems leadership that required thinking beyond individual and team leadership to understanding how leaders can influence entire organizational systems. Peter Senge's work on learning organizations provided frameworks for creating systems that continuously adapt and improve rather than merely maintaining current performance levels.

The implementation of systems thinking required developing new capabilities for seeing patterns across time and organizational levels, understanding feedback loops and unintended consequences, and intervening in ways that created sustainable positive change. This was significantly different from military approaches that often focused on immediate tactical results rather than long-term system health.

The practical application of systems leadership principles included initiatives such as cross-functional collaboration improvement, organizational learning system development, and culture change efforts that addressed root causes rather than symptoms. These initiatives required patience, persistence, and tolerance for gradual change that challenged my military training in rapid decisive action.

Organizational development work became a significant focus area, requiring skills in change management, culture assessment, and systematic intervention design. The integration of organization development theory with practical experience provided tools for diagnosing organizational challenges and designing interventions that addressed underlying system dynamics rather than surface symptoms.

Research Foundation Development

A significant portion of year two involved developing the foundation for doctoral research while continuing to focus on practical leadership development. The identification of resilience development through direct feedback as a potential dissertation focus required systematic exploration of existing research literature while simultaneously experimenting with feedback-based resilience development approaches in my current work environment.

The literature review process revealed significant gaps in understanding how structured feedback systems can be designed to systematically develop both individual and organizational resilience capabilities. Most resilience research focused on individual psychological factors or organizational policies and procedures, but little attention had been paid to how feedback systems could be designed to enhance adaptive capacity at multiple organizational levels.

The development of research questions required integrating academic theoretical frameworks with practical organizational challenges. The military experience provided unique perspectives on resilience under extreme conditions, but the civilian organizational context required different approaches to resilience development that could be sustainable and effective in less intense environments.

Preliminary research design work included consideration of mixed-methods approaches that could capture both quantitative resilience indicators and qualitative understanding of how feedback systems influence resilience development. The integration of military experience with academic research methods provided opportunities for innovative approaches to resilience research that could have practical applications across different organizational contexts.

Leadership Coaching and Development

Year two included significant focus on developing coaching and leadership development capabilities. The transition from directive leadership approaches to coaching-based development required new skills in questioning, listening, and facilitating self-discovery rather than providing solutions and direction.

The International Coach Federation competency framework provided structure for developing coaching capabilities, but the integration with leadership practice required careful attention to when coaching approaches were appropriate versus when more directive leadership was necessary. Military experience had emphasized clear direction and rapid execution, but civilian leadership contexts often benefited from coaching approaches that built capability and engagement rather than simple compliance.

The application of coaching skills to leadership development within my current organization required systematic practice and feedback. Regular coaching conversations with team members focused on their development goals, career aspirations, and skill building rather than just task completion and performance management.

Mentoring relationships with other leaders in the organization provided opportunities to practice coaching skills while also receiving feedback on leadership effectiveness. These relationships proved valuable for both giving and receiving support for leadership development challenges.

The exploration of interest-based negotiation and conflict resolution provided tools for understanding underlying needs and concerns rather than just addressing surface positions. The Harvard Negotiation Project methodology became a framework for approaching difficult conversations with curiosity and collaboration rather than defensiveness or aggression.

Restorative justice principles provided additional tools for addressing conflict in ways that repaired relationships and built stronger team dynamics. The application of these principles required moving beyond punishment-based approaches to mistake-making toward learning-based approaches that built stronger systems and relationships.

The practice of difficult conversations became a regular part of leadership development, with systematic attention to preparation, emotional regulation during the conversation, and follow-up to ensure that desired outcomes were achieved. These skills proved essential for maintaining high performance standards while building trust and engagement.

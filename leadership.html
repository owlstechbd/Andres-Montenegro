Personal Leadership Development Plan

The Personal Leadership Development Plan is structured around four core areas: Resilience Development, Emotional Intelligence, Psychological Safety, and Direct Feedback. The objective is to integrate these principles into daily leadership practices, creating an environment where both leaders and team members feel empowered to contribute effectively.

Resilience Development

Focus on strengthening decision-making under pressure by implementing strategies that promote adaptive thinking and maintaining composure in high-stakes situations.

Psychological Safety

:Establish a culture where team members feel safe to express ideas, share concerns, and offer constructive feedback without fear of retaliation or judgment. This includes promoting open dialogue, encouraging diverse perspectives, and creating an inclusive atmosphere that values input from all team members.

Emotional Intelligence

Develop the ability to manage emotions effectively, fostering deeper connections with team members and enhancing communication in diverse settings.

Direct Feedback

Utilize structured feedback sessions to provide clear, actionable insights that drive personal and professional growth, ensuring that feedback is delivered in a way that is constructive, supportive, and aimed at fostering continuous improvement.

This development plan is designed to cultivate a leadership style that not only prioritizes strategic decision-making but also emphasizes creating a psychologically safe workplace where innovation, trust, and growth are actively encouraged.

Year 1 Reflection - Leadership Development Journey

Doctor of Executive Leadership Program - University of Charleston, WV

Foundation and Context: Military Leadership Meets Academic Framework

Entering the Doctor of Executive Leadership program marked a profound transition from operational military leadership to academic exploration of leadership theory and practice. My eighteen years of military service, including specialized operations and international experience with the French Foreign Legion, provided a unique lens through which to examine and challenge traditional leadership paradigms. Year one became a critical period of deconstructing previously held assumptions about leadership while simultaneously building a more nuanced, research-based understanding of what it means to lead effectively in complex, dynamic environments.

The military context had instilled in me a command-and-control mindset that emphasized hierarchy, rapid decision-making under pressure, and results-oriented approaches to team management. However, the DEL program immediately challenged this framework by introducing concepts that required a more collaborative, emotionally intelligent, and psychologically aware approach to leadership. This cognitive dissonance became the foundation for significant personal and professional growth throughout the first year.

Theoretical Frameworks and Paradigm Shifts

The introduction to contemporary leadership theories created a fundamental shift in my understanding of effective leadership practice. Transformational leadership theory, particularly James MacGregor Burns' original conceptualization and Bass's subsequent refinements, provided a framework that resonated deeply with my military experience while challenging me to evolve beyond transactional approaches.

Adaptive leadership, as conceptualized by Ronald Heifetz and Marty Linsky, became particularly relevant given my background in special operations where adaptability under extreme conditions was essential for survival and mission success. However, the academic exploration of adaptive leadership revealed layers of complexity I had not previously considered.

The concept of distributed leadership challenged my deeply ingrained beliefs about hierarchical command structures. In military contexts, clear chains of command are not merely organizational preferences but operational necessities that can mean the difference between mission success and catastrophic failure.

Emotional Intelligence: The Missing Dimension

Perhaps the most transformative aspect of year one was the deep exploration of emotional intelligence as a leadership competency. Daniel Goleman's framework of self-awareness, self-regulation, motivation, empathy, and social skills provided a systematic approach to understanding and developing capabilities that had been largely intuitive in my military experience.

Self-awareness became a particular focus of development. The program included extensive self-assessment tools, including the Emotional Quotient Inventory (EQ-i 2.0), 360-degree feedback instruments, and structured reflection exercises that revealed patterns in my emotional responses and leadership behaviors.

The development of empathy as a leadership competency required significant personal work. Military training emphasizes mission accomplishment and troop welfare, but the systematic development of empathetic understanding—the ability to truly comprehend and share the feelings of others—required new skills and perspectives.

Key Insight from Year One

"The integration of military experience with academic learning created new perspectives on leadership effectiveness and new capabilities for influencing positive change in organizations and communities."

Psychological Safety: Creating Space for Excellence

The introduction of Amy Edmondson's research on psychological safety represented one of the most practically relevant concepts encountered in year one. Psychological safety—the belief that one can speak up without risk of punishment or humiliation—directly challenged aspects of traditional military culture while simultaneously explaining why some military units consistently outperformed others.

Implementing psychological safety concepts required careful consideration of context and culture. In military environments, certain types of psychological safety are essential for operational effectiveness—team members must feel comfortable reporting equipment malfunctions, tactical concerns, or personal limitations that could affect mission success.

Looking Forward: Foundation for Continued Growth

Year one established a solid foundation for continued leadership development throughout the DEL program. The combination of theoretical learning, practical application, systematic feedback, and reflective practice created a framework for ongoing growth that could be sustained beyond the academic program.

The identification of resilience development through direct feedback as a potential dissertation focus emerged from year one learning and reflection. The integration of military experience with academic learning suggested that structured feedback systems could be powerful tools for developing both individual and organizational resilience capabilities.

The year concluded with a clear sense of direction for continued development and a commitment to integrating academic learning with practical leadership challenges. The foundation established in year one provided confidence that the subsequent years of the program would build effectively on this base while continuing to challenge and expand leadership capabilities.

Year 2 Reflection - Leadership Development Journey

Doctor of Executive Leadership Program - University of Charleston, WV

Transition from Theory to Praxis: The Integration Challenge

Year two of the Doctor of Executive Leadership program represented a fundamental shift from theoretical exploration to practical integration and application. While year one had focused on deconstructing existing leadership assumptions and building new theoretical frameworks, year two challenged me to synthesize these learnings into coherent, actionable leadership practices while simultaneously laying the groundwork for doctoral research.

The military background that had informed my year one learning continued to provide valuable perspective, but year two required moving beyond the adaptation of military leadership principles to the creation of entirely new leadership approaches suited to complex civilian organizational contexts. This evolution demanded not just intellectual understanding but embodied change—the development of new leadership habits, reflexes, and capabilities.

Advanced Leadership Theory Integration

Year two introduced more sophisticated theoretical frameworks that required deeper integration with practical experience. The exploration of complexity theory and its application to leadership challenged fundamental assumptions about control, predictability, and linear cause-and-effect relationships that had been central to military leadership training.

Adaptive leadership theory became a central focus for practical application. Ronald Heifetz's distinction between technical problems and adaptive challenges provided a lens for analyzing current leadership challenges in my work environment. The systematic application of adaptive leadership principles became daily practices that fundamentally changed how I approached organizational challenges.

Emotional Intelligence Mastery and Application

Building on year one's foundation in emotional intelligence theory, year two focused on advanced application and mastery of emotional leadership competencies. The systematic development of emotional intelligence moved beyond self-awareness to sophisticated understanding of group emotional dynamics and the leader's role in managing collective emotional states.

The practical application of emotional intelligence principles required significant behavior change. Military culture had taught emotional regulation through suppression and compartmentalization, but civilian leadership contexts required more sophisticated approaches to emotional expression and management.

Psychological Safety Implementation

Amy Edmondson's research on psychological safety became a central focus for practical implementation in year two. The challenge was creating systematic approaches for building and maintaining psychological safety within existing organizational cultures that might not naturally support such environments.

Specific practices developed included structured team reflection sessions, systematic error analysis focused on learning rather than blame, regular one-on-one check-ins, and modeling vulnerability by openly discussing my own mistakes and learning processes.

Advanced Communication Strategies

Year two required moving beyond basic communication competencies to sophisticated understanding of influence, persuasion, and communication across different organizational levels and contexts. The integration of neuroscience research on decision-making and influence provided new tools.

The exploration of narrative leadership became particularly important. Stories and metaphors proved powerful tools for creating shared understanding, building emotional connections, and inspiring action in ways that traditional military communication approaches could not achieve.

Key Insight from Year Two

"The integration of advanced theoretical frameworks with intensive practical application created leadership capabilities that were both sophisticated and authentic, blending military discipline with academic insights for organizational impact."

Systems Leadership and Organizational Development

Year two introduced advanced concepts in systems leadership that required thinking beyond individual and team leadership to understanding how leaders can influence entire organizational systems. Peter Senge's work on learning organizations provided frameworks for creating systems that continuously adapt and improve.

The practical application of systems leadership principles included initiatives such as cross-functional collaboration improvement, organizational learning system development, and culture change efforts that addressed root causes rather than symptoms.

Research Foundation Development

A significant portion of year two involved developing the foundation for doctoral research while continuing to focus on practical leadership development. The identification of resilience development through direct feedback as a potential dissertation focus required systematic exploration of existing research literature.

The literature review process revealed significant gaps in understanding how structured feedback systems can be designed to systematically develop both individual and organizational resilience capabilities.

Integration and Synthesis: Advanced Leadership Philosophy

Year two culminated in the refinement and advancement of the personal leadership philosophy developed in year one. The integration of advanced theoretical frameworks with intensive practical application created a more sophisticated and nuanced approach to leadership.

The advanced leadership philosophy incorporated several key refinements: Adaptive Complexity Leadership, Integrated Emotional and Systems Intelligence, Cultural Intelligence Application, Resilience-Based Leadership, and Feedback-Driven Development.

Practical Applications and Outcomes

Year two resulted in significant practical improvements in leadership effectiveness within my current organizational context. Systematic feedback indicated measurable improvements in areas such as team engagement, innovation, psychological safety, and overall performance outcomes.

Specific initiatives implemented during year two included: Team Development Program, Organizational Learning System, Leadership Development Initiative, and Culture Change Effort.
